Tutorial: Explaining the Data Science Pipeline¶

Introduction¶

In simple terms, Data science is mainly about analysing structured or unstructured data, and extracting insight from the data. Data science is guided by a process commonly referred to as the Data Science Pipeline. In this tutorial, you will learn about the steps involved in the data science pipleine, namely: data collection/curation, data management and representation, expoloratory data analysis (EDA), hypothesis testing and machine learning. I will walk you through each of these steps using a dataset from the COVID tracking project that shows covid cases in Maryland. We will use this dataset to find out if there is a relation between covid cases, deaths, hospitalization and time.

Goal: By the end of this tutorial, you would have obtained insight on how the datascience pipeline works, and you will be able to utilize this to perform proper data science on any data.

Data representation:¶

  • date- Date for which the daily totals were collected
  • positive- Total number of people who have tested positive for COVID-19 so far
  • negative- Total number of people who have negative positive for COVID-19 so far
  • hospitalized_currently- Number of people in hospital for COVID-19 on this day
  • hospitalized_cumulative- tal number of people who have gone to the hospital for COVID-19 so far, including those who have since recovered or died.
  • in_icu_currently- Total number of people in the ICU for COVID-19 on this day
  • in_icu_cumulative- Total number of people who have gone to the ICU for COVID-19 so far, including those who have since recovered or died
  • recovered- Total number of people who have recovered from COVID-19 so far.
  • death- Total number of people who have died as a result of COVID-19 so far.

Dealing with missing data¶

That looks better, but it looks like we have a lot of missing data. Missing data in our table could lead to complications when performing row operations and could possibly yield wrong results if not handled properly. So it is important to check for missing data and deal with it prior to performing analysis.

  • I'll use the missingno library to visualize missing data. Then, based on that, I'll determine how to deal with the missing data.

EDA: Exploratory Data Analysis¶

In this phase of the data pipeline, this is where we make visual representations of our data and try to find patterns. In this phase we may try to view the data from multiple angles to see if we can come up with hypotheses. This is important because without a hypothesis, we might not know what to expect from the data. Our hypotheses might not always be true, but it most cases, they help to uncover more information about the data. This is where some visualizations of our data come in handy

Remember, our aim is to find out if there is a relation between covid cases, deaths, hospitalization, and what that relationship is? Perhpars there's a trend that varies with time?.

Null Hypothesis: There's no relationship between age groups and other factors like race/ethnicity for people who tested positivefor COVID in Maryland.

Machine learning: Analysing Recovery¶

According to the heatmap, there is a strong corellation between recovery rate and hospitalization. I'm going to evaluate if this is the case.

  • First we'll train a linear regression using the number of positive, negative and death cases only.
  • Then we'll put add hospitalization as an interaction term.
In [211]:
